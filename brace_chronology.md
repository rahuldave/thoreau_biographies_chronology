# Charles Loring Brace Chronology

Source keys are defined in `the_book_that_changed_america_chronology.md`.
Brace is one of the four people at Sanborn's January 1, 1860 Origin dinner.

## Chronology

- **June 19, 1826 - Brace is born in Litchfield, Connecticut.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Brace.
  - Summary: Brace was born into a New England family that connected him to Protestant reform, education, and antislavery politics. Those networks later help explain why he can move between New York social work, Asa Gray, and the Concord circle.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace.

- **1846 - Brace earns a B.A. from Yale College.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Brace.
  - Summary: Brace completed his Yale degree before turning toward ministry, travel, and reform work. The education placed him inside the elite intellectual world that later made Darwinian argument useful to him.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace.

- **1848 - Brace moves to New York.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F4; W-Brace.
  - Summary: Brace moved to New York and began seeing urban poverty at close range. That move shifted him from inherited New England respectability toward practical work among poor and immigrant children.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 4: Word of Mouth (text book.md:373-450; notes book.md:1937-2014).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace.

- **1852 - Brace works among the poor of Blackwell's Island and the Five Points Mission.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F4; W-Brace.
  - Summary: Brace worked directly with incarcerated and destitute people at Blackwell's Island and in the Five Points neighborhood. These encounters supplied the reform problem he later tried to interpret through environment, inheritance, and social struggle.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 4: Word of Mouth (text book.md:373-450; notes book.md:1937-2014).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace.

- **1853 - Brace founds the Children's Aid Society.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F4; W-Brace, W-ChildrensAid.
  - Summary: Brace founded the Children's Aid Society to organize lodging, schooling, job placement, and relocation for poor children. The institution became the main vehicle for his long reform career.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 4: Word of Mouth (text book.md:373-450; notes book.md:1937-2014).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace; Wikipedia, Children's Aid.

- **August 21, 1854 - Brace marries Letitia Neill in Belfast.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Brace.
  - Summary: Brace married Letitia Neill while traveling in Ireland. The marriage joined his reform life to a household that would later be present in the background of his New York and transatlantic work.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace.

- **1854 - Children's Aid opens newsboys' lodging houses and begins placing children outside New York.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F4; W-Brace, W-ChildrensAid, W-OrphanTrain.
  - Summary: The Children's Aid Society began housing newsboys and placing poor children with families outside the city. This was the practical beginning of the relocation system later known as the orphan trains.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 4: Word of Mouth (text book.md:373-450; notes book.md:1937-2014).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace; Wikipedia, Children's Aid; Wikipedia, Orphan Train.

- **September-October 1854 - The first large orphan-train expedition reaches the Midwest.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-OrphanTrain.
  - Summary: Brace and the Children's Aid Society sent a group of New York children west to be placed with rural families. The expedition became an early model for the Society's large-scale child relocation program.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Orphan Train.

- **January 1, 1860 - Brace brings Asa Gray's annotated London copy of Origin to Sanborn's Concord dinner with Thoreau and Bronson Alcott.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F1, F4; W-Brace, W-AsaGray, W-Origin.
  - Summary: Brace carried Asa Gray's marked London copy of Origin to Sanborn's New Year dinner in Concord. That act physically brought Darwin's book into the room with Thoreau, Alcott, and Sanborn.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 1: The Book from Across the Atlantic (text book.md:235-294; notes book.md:1847-1886); Chapter 4: Word of Mouth (text book.md:373-450; notes book.md:1937-2014).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace; Wikipedia, Asa Gray; Wikipedia, On the Origin of Species.

- **January 2, 1860 - Brace returns Gray's copy and goes back to New York.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F4; W-Brace, W-AsaGray.
  - Summary: After the Concord dinner, Brace returned Gray's copy and went back to New York. The book did not stay with him, but the argument immediately started working inside his reform imagination.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 4: Word of Mouth (text book.md:373-450; notes book.md:1937-2014).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace; Wikipedia, Asa Gray.

- **Early January 1860 - Brace visits Dutch Hill after reading Darwin and begins interpreting poverty through struggle, environment, and inheritance.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F4; W-Brace, W-ChildrensAid.
  - Summary: Brace visited the Dutch Hill district after reading Darwin and began linking poverty to struggle, environment, heredity, and adaptation. The scene made Origin feel applicable to city life rather than only to natural history.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 4: Word of Mouth (text book.md:373-450; notes book.md:1937-2014).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace; Wikipedia, Children's Aid.

- **Early January 1860 - Brace sees Barnum's "What-Is-It?" advertisements and turns Origin toward ethnology and antislavery race argument.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F10; W-Barnum, W-Zip, W-Brace.
  - Summary: Barnum's advertisements for the displayed Black performer known as the "What-Is-It?" pushed Brace toward questions of race, human descent, and antislavery argument. He began using Darwin against fixed racial hierarchy rather than for it.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 10: The What-Is-It? (text book.md:763-830; notes book.md:2315-2382).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, P. T. Barnum; Wikipedia, Zip the Pinhead; Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace.

- **January-February 1860 - Brace becomes the first American writer in Fuller's account to develop an ethnological interpretation of Darwin.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F10, F13; W-Brace, W-Douglass, W-Agassiz.
  - Summary: Brace developed an early American attempt to connect Darwinian common descent with ethnology. In Fuller's account, he becomes one of the first American readers to make Origin speak directly to race.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 10: The What-Is-It? (text book.md:763-830; notes book.md:2315-2382); Chapter 13: Tree of Life (text book.md:959-1006; notes book.md:2477-2518).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace; Wikipedia, Frederick Douglass; Wikipedia, Louis Agassiz.

- **July 1860 - Brace reads Gray's Atlantic essay and reorganizes his race thinking around language and descent.** Check: Fuller-confirmed for Brace's reading; web-confirmed for Gray's essay. Sources: F13; Atlantic-Gray1860, W-AsaGray, W-Brace.
  - Summary: Brace read Gray's Atlantic essay and found a more disciplined way to think about descent, language, and human variation. Gray's public defense of Darwin helped Brace organize his own antislavery ethnology.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 13: Tree of Life (text book.md:959-1006; notes book.md:2477-2518).
  - Web context: Asa Gray, "Darwin on the Origin of Species," The Atlantic, July 1860; Wikipedia, Asa Gray; Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace.

- **Autumn 1860 - Brace studies linguistics and sends his manuscript to Gray, who criticizes it.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F13, F20; W-Brace, W-AsaGray.
  - Summary: Brace studied language families and sent Gray a manuscript that tried to combine linguistics, race, and Darwinian descent. Gray pushed back, showing how ambitious and unstable Brace's Darwinian extrapolation could be.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 13: Tree of Life (text book.md:959-1006; notes book.md:2477-2518); Chapter 20: Races of the Old World (text book.md:1439-1494; notes book.md:2765-2806).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace; Wikipedia, Asa Gray.

- **1863 - Brace publishes The Races of the Old World.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F20, F22; W-Brace.
  - Summary: Brace published The Races of the Old World, arguing against separate human creations and for a shared human ancestry. The book made his Darwin-inflected antislavery ethnology public.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 20: Races of the Old World (text book.md:1439-1494; notes book.md:2765-2806); Chapter 22: At Down House (text book.md:1589-1656; notes book.md:2875-2932).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace.

- **1863 - New York draft riots expose the racial and class violence surrounding Brace's reform world.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F22; W-NYDraftRiots.
  - Summary: The New York draft riots brought racial terror and class violence into the streets around Brace's reform world. The events exposed the limits and urgency of his project among the city's poor.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 22: At Down House (text book.md:1589-1656; notes book.md:2875-2932).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, New York City draft riots.

- **1869 - Brace writes Henry Ward Beecher about slow moral growth.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F22; W-Brace.
  - Summary: Brace wrote to Henry Ward Beecher in language of gradual development and moral growth. By this point Darwinian and evolutionary vocabulary had become part of how he imagined social reform.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 22: At Down House (text book.md:1589-1656; notes book.md:2875-2932).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace.

- **1870 - Brace writes on evolution in the North American Review and Christian Union.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F22; W-Brace, W-Origin.
  - Summary: Brace wrote about evolution for major American periodicals, translating Darwinian ideas for religious and reform-minded readers. These essays show his continued effort to join evolution, Christianity, and social improvement.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 22: At Down House (text book.md:1589-1656; notes book.md:2875-2932).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace; Wikipedia, On the Origin of Species.

- **1872 - Brace publishes The Dangerous Classes of New York and Twenty Years' Work Among Them.** Check: web-confirmed context. Sources: W-Brace.
  - Summary: Brace published a major account of New York poverty, crime, child welfare, and the Children's Aid Society's work. The book became a summary of the reform world that had first made Darwin socially useful to him.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace.

- **July 1872 - Brace visits Darwin at Down House.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F22; W-Brace, W-Darwin.
  - Summary: Brace visited Darwin at Down House and met the man whose theory had helped reshape his thinking about race and reform. The visit closes a transatlantic loop begun with Gray's copy of Origin.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 22: At Down House (text book.md:1589-1656; notes book.md:2875-2932).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace; Wikipedia, Charles Darwin.

- **August 11, 1890 - Brace dies.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Brace.
  - Summary: Brace died after decades of child-welfare and urban reform work. His chronology ends with Darwinism folded into the longer legacy of the Children's Aid Society.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Charles Loring Brace.

## Arc in Fuller

Brace reads Origin through social reform and race. His chronology should not be
flattened into simple progress: Fuller repeatedly notes that Brace uses Darwin
against polygenesis and slavery while retaining racial hierarchy and
colonizationist assumptions.
