# Ralph Waldo Emerson Chronology

Source keys are defined in `the_book_that_changed_america_chronology.md`.
Emerson was not one of the four people at Sanborn's January 1, 1860 dinner, but
Fuller's Concord story repeatedly depends on Emerson's house, library, family,
Saturday Club circle, and influence on Thoreau, Sanborn, and Alcott.

## Chronology

- **May 25, 1803 - Emerson is born in Boston.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Emerson.
  - Summary: Emerson was born in Boston into a family shaped by ministry and New England letters. His later role in Fuller's story is as the older intellectual center of Concord.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **October 1817 - Emerson enters Harvard College.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Emerson.
  - Summary: Emerson entered Harvard as a young student. Harvard gave him the institutional and literary grounding from which his ministry and later authorship emerged.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **August 29, 1821 - Emerson graduates from Harvard.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Emerson.
  - Summary: Emerson graduated from Harvard and moved into teaching, ministry preparation, and writing. The degree marks the beginning of his public intellectual formation.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **January 11, 1829 - Emerson is ordained at Boston's Second Church.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Emerson.
  - Summary: Emerson was ordained as minister of Boston's Second Church. His later resignation from this role made his move away from inherited theology especially visible.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **February 8, 1831 - Ellen Louisa Tucker Emerson dies.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Emerson.
  - Summary: Ellen Tucker Emerson died of tuberculosis after a short marriage. Her death deepened Emerson's religious crisis and helped push him toward a different intellectual life.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **1832 - Emerson resigns from the ministry.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Emerson.
  - Summary: Emerson resigned from the ministry after doubts about doctrine and communion. The decision opened the path toward lecturing, essays, and Transcendentalism.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **1833 - Emerson tours Europe and visits the Jardin des Plantes in Paris, an experience later linked to his movement from theology toward science and nature.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Emerson.
  - Summary: Emerson toured Europe and visited scientific collections in Paris, including the Jardin des Plantes. Fuller treats the encounter with natural order and classification as part of Emerson's shift toward nature and science.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **1835 - Emerson settles in Concord and marries Lydia/Lidian Jackson.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Emerson.
  - Summary: Emerson settled in Concord and married Lydia Jackson, known as Lidian. His house became a gathering place for the circle that later receives news of Brown and Darwin.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **September 1836 - Emerson publishes Nature and helps launch the Transcendental Club.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Emerson, W-Nature.
  - Summary: Emerson published Nature and helped launch the Transcendental Club. These events made him a central figure in the movement that shaped Thoreau and Alcott.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson; Wikipedia, Nature.

- **August 31, 1837 - Emerson delivers "The American Scholar."** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Emerson.
  - Summary: Emerson delivered The American Scholar, calling for intellectual independence from European models. The address became one of his signature statements of American literary self-reliance.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **Fall 1837 - Emerson asks Thoreau whether he keeps a journal, helping catalyze Thoreau's lifelong practice.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F7; W-Emerson, W-Thoreau.
  - Summary: Emerson asked Thoreau whether he kept a journal. The question helped prompt the journal practice that later preserved Thoreau's natural-history observations and Darwinian reading.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7: The Nick of Time (text book.md:581-658; notes book.md:2141-2218).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson; Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau.

- **July 15, 1838 - Emerson delivers the Divinity School Address.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Emerson, W-DivinityAddress.
  - Summary: Emerson delivered the Divinity School Address and provoked controversy with its challenge to inherited Christianity. The speech confirmed him as a radical religious and literary figure.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson; Wikipedia, Divinity School Address.

- **1841 - Emerson publishes Essays: First Series.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Emerson.
  - Summary: Emerson published Essays: First Series, including some of his most influential essays. The collection established the mature voice of his Transcendental thought.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **1844 - Emerson publishes Essays: Second Series.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Emerson.
  - Summary: Emerson published Essays: Second Series, extending his philosophical and literary authority. By the 1840s he had become the figure around whom Concord intellectual life gathered.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **July 4, 1845 - Thoreau moves to Walden Pond on Emerson's land.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F7; W-Emerson, W-Thoreau, W-Walden.

## Arc in Fuller

Emerson is the gravitational center of Concord rather than the most direct
Darwinian reader. Fuller uses him to stage the tension between Transcendental
idealism and Darwinian naturalism: Emerson is curious and receptive, but it is
Thoreau who turns the shock of Origin into sustained ecological practice.
