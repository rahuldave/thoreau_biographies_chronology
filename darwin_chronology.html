Darwin Chronology

Charles Darwin
Aligned timeline
February 12, 1809

Charles Darwin is born in Shrewsbury, England.

Summary: Darwin was born into a prosperous English family with medical, scientific, and reform connections. Fuller needs the birth only as the start of the life that will eventually produce Origin.

Evidence: Darwin's birth anchors the scientific life behind Origin, but the main chronology only needs it as a starting point for Fuller's prehistory of the theory. Fuller introduces Darwin through the habits that later matter: collecting, patient comparison, and reluctance to publish before the evidence is overwhelming.

Book anchor: Chapter 3: Beetles, Birds, Theories places Darwin's formation behind the shock Americans feel in 1859-1860.

Web context: Wikipedia's Darwin biography supplies the public life dates and identifies natural selection as Darwin's central scientific contribution.

1825-1827

Darwin studies medicine at Edinburgh, then moves toward Cambridge and natural history.

Summary: Darwin tried medical study at Edinburgh, disliked much of it, and moved toward Cambridge and natural history. The detour pushed him away from a conventional profession and toward collecting, observing, and correspondence.

Evidence: Fuller treats these years as a turn away from conventional professional training and toward natural-history practice. The shift helps explain why Origin arrives as an evidentiary argument built from specimens, field observation, and correspondence.

Book anchor: Chapter 3 uses this educational detour to set up Darwin's later authority as an observer.

December 27, 1831-October 2, 1836

Darwin sails on HMS Beagle and returns with evidence that species may not be fixed.

Summary: Darwin sailed as a naturalist on HMS Beagle and gathered fossils, specimens, and distribution evidence across the globe. The voyage supplied the observations that later made fixed species seem doubtful to him.

Evidence: The Beagle voyage is the field base for the theory Fuller follows into America. Geographic distribution, fossils, and island variation become the observations that later make Origin persuasive to readers already aware of Darwin as a travel writer and naturalist.

Book anchor: Chapters 3, 6, and 7 use the Beagle background to connect Darwin's theory to Gray, Brace, and Thoreau's reading.

Web context: Darwin and Origin summaries identify the voyage as the empirical seedbed for his later evolutionary argument.

1837

Darwin begins systematic notebooks on species transmutation.

Summary: Darwin opened notebooks on transmutation after the Beagle voyage. In them, he began turning scattered observations into a systematic inquiry into species change.

Evidence: The notebooks mark the point where Darwin's observations become an explicit inquiry into species change. Fuller uses this long private development to distinguish Origin from sudden speculation.

Late 1838

Darwin reads Malthus and clarifies the struggle-for-existence logic of natural selection.

Summary: Darwin read Malthus and saw how population pressure could produce a struggle for existence. That insight helped him formulate natural selection as a mechanism rather than only a suspicion that species changed.

Evidence: The Malthus moment is essential to Fuller's American story because "struggle" later travels from biological explanation into social, racial, and political interpretation. It helps explain why American readers read Origin as more than a technical book about species.

Book anchor: Chapters 3 and 10 connect Darwin's mechanism with later American arguments about competition, race, and survival.

Web context: Public summaries of Origin describe struggle for existence as one of the theory's key inferences.

1842-1844

Darwin drafts and expands private sketches of his species theory.

Summary: Darwin wrote private sketches of his theory but kept them unpublished. These drafts show that the theory was mature long before Origin, which is why Wallace's 1858 manuscript created a priority crisis.

Evidence: Fuller treats the sketches as evidence of delay, caution, and priority. They also show why Wallace's 1858 manuscript becomes a crisis: Darwin has a mature theory but has not yet made it public.

Book anchor: Chapter 3 treats the sketches as the private groundwork behind Origin.

Web context: The Origin page describes the 1842 sketch and 1844 essay as key stages in Darwin's publication history.

1855

Darwin sends Asa Gray an abstract of natural selection.

Summary: Darwin sent Asa Gray an abstract of natural selection before the theory was public. The letter later helped establish priority and made Gray a crucial American confidant.

Evidence: This is the one Gray event that belongs on Darwin's sparse line because it bears directly on Darwin's priority and on the transatlantic route by which the theory reaches America. Fuller uses the letter as documentary ballast when Wallace independently arrives at a similar theory.

Book anchor: Chapter 3 highlights this letter as a transatlantic anchor for Darwin's claim.

June-July 1858

Wallace's Ternate manuscript prompts the joint Darwin-Wallace presentation at the Linnean Society.

Summary: Wallace independently sent Darwin a manuscript that described natural selection. Darwin's friends arranged a joint Linnean Society presentation, moving the theory into public scientific record.

Evidence: Wallace's manuscript forces Darwin's private theory into public circulation. Fuller uses the episode to explain why Origin is both urgent and carefully positioned when it appears the next year.

Book anchor: Chapter 3 makes the Wallace episode the turning point from private theory to public priority problem.

December 1858-Spring 1859

Asa Gray begins using Darwinian descent against Agassiz's fixed-species geography.

Summary: Gray used Darwinian descent to challenge Agassiz's claim that plant distributions reflected separate creations. The debate made Darwin's unpublished theory an active weapon in American science before Origin appeared.

Evidence: This stays sparse on Darwin's line, but it matters because Darwin's unpublished theory already has an American advocate before Origin appears. Gray's plant-geography argument helps explain why he becomes the book's most important American defender.

Book anchor: Chapter 3 gives Darwin's earlier botanical questions to Gray; Chapter 11 shows Gray later turning those methods against Agassiz.

November 24, 1859

On the Origin of Species is published in London.

Summary: Origin was published in London and quickly sold out. The book converted Darwin's long private theory into the public controversy that Fuller follows across the Atlantic.

Evidence: This is the hinge event for Fuller's book: the object that moves from London to Cambridge, from Asa Gray to Brace, and from Brace into Sanborn's Concord dinner. The publication date anchors the book chronology against later chapter-order flashbacks and afterlives.

Book anchor: Chapters 1 and 3 establish the publication as both a scientific event and a physical book in transatlantic circulation.

Web context: Wikipedia and Darwin Online corroborate the title, first publication date, and bibliographic status of Origin.

Mid-January 1860

The Appleton American edition of Origin appears.

Summary: Appleton's American edition made Origin available beyond the few imported London copies. In Concord, that meant Thoreau could borrow and annotate the book for sustained reading.

Evidence: The American edition changes Origin from a scarce imported book into something Thoreau can borrow from the Concord library. Fuller uses this publication shift to explain why Thoreau's Darwin reading intensifies after the New Year's dinner.

Book anchor: Chapters 1, 6, 7, and 14 trace the path from Gray's London copy to Thoreau's Appleton copy.

Web context: The Origin page describes the American publication as part of the book's rapid spread outside Britain.

Summer 1860

Darwin continues experimental work at Down House while American readers debate Origin.

Evidence: Fuller places Darwin's continuing research beside the American reception to show that Darwinism is not only a controversy abroad but also an ongoing experimental program at home. The contrast is especially sharp beside Thoreau's field observations in Concord.

1868

Darwin publishes Variation, challenging Gray's design position.

Summary: Darwin published Variation of Animals and Plants under Domestication, extending his evidence for descent and heredity. The work increased pressure on Gray's attempt to reconcile Darwin with providential design.

1871

Darwin publishes The Descent of Man.

Summary: Darwin published The Descent of Man and applied evolutionary reasoning explicitly to humans. The book made unavoidable many of the race and ancestry questions that American readers had already drawn from Origin.

Evidence: Descent makes explicit the human implications that American readers like Brace had already begun drawing from Origin. Fuller uses it to show that race, common descent, and human ancestry were not peripheral to Darwin's American reception.

Book anchor: Chapters 20 and 22 connect Brace's race manuscript and Darwin's later human-evolution argument.
