# John Brown Chronology

Source keys are defined in `the_book_that_changed_america_chronology.md`.
This optional line tracks Brown and the abolition crisis because Fuller's
Darwin story repeatedly crosses the shock of Harpers Ferry, Concord radicalism,
and Civil War memory.

## Chronology

- **May 9, 1800 - John Brown is born in Torrington, Connecticut.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: Brown's birth is included only to orient the optional line; Fuller's book becomes interested in Brown much later, when his antislavery militancy transforms Concord's moral atmosphere just as Origin arrives.
  - Web context: Wikipedia's Brown biography supplies the life dates and identifies him with radical abolitionism, Bleeding Kansas, and Harpers Ferry.

- **1854 - The Kansas-Nebraska Act and Bleeding Kansas widen the slavery crisis.** Check: web-confirmed; Fuller-confirmed for Sanborn/Brown background. Sources: F5, F7; W-KansasNebraska, W-BleedingKansas, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: Fuller uses Kansas as the political world that radicalizes Sanborn and makes Brown more than a distant abolitionist. The same crisis helps explain why Concord readers receive Origin in an atmosphere already charged with violence, race, and national rupture.
  - Book anchor: Chapters 5 and 7 put Brown's Kansas world behind Sanborn and Thoreau's later responses.
  - Web context: Public web sources anchor Kansas-Nebraska, Bleeding Kansas, and Brown's role in that conflict.

- **1856 - Brown gains national notoriety through militant action in Kansas.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F5, F7; W-BleedingKansas, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: Fuller does not need every Kansas incident for the chronology, but Brown's turn toward antislavery violence explains why he becomes such a polarizing figure by 1859. Concord radicals later see him through this lens of prophetic action rather than ordinary politics.
  - Book anchor: Chapters 5 and 7 use Brown's Kansas reputation to prepare the Harpers Ferry response.
  - Web context: Brown and Bleeding Kansas sources summarize his national prominence in the 1850s.

- **1858-1859 - Brown receives support from the men later remembered as the Secret Six, including Franklin Sanborn.** Check: web-confirmed; Fuller-confirmed for Sanborn's role. Sources: F5, F16; W-SecretSix, W-Sanborn, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: The Secret Six connection is central because it pulls Brown directly into the Concord network of Sanborn, Thoreau, Emerson, and the Alcotts. Fuller uses it to show that the Origin dinner takes place in a house already under pressure from federal investigation.
  - Book anchor: Chapters 5 and 16 make Sanborn's Brown connection a live political danger, not a retrospective footnote.
  - Web context: The Secret Six source identifies Sanborn as one of Brown's supporters and explains the later name for the support network.

- **June 1859 - Sanborn expects Brown's antislavery action to begin soon.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F5; W-SecretSix, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: Fuller's point is anticipatory: Sanborn knows Brown's plan is nearing action before the public knows anything. That foreknowledge makes the later Senate summons and arrest attempt intelligible.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 5 places Sanborn inside the tense months before Harpers Ferry.
  - Web context: Brown and Secret Six sources supply the larger public context for the plan.

- **October 16-18, 1859 - Brown raids Harpers Ferry.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F1, F5, F7; W-JohnBrownRaid, Battlefields-JohnBrown, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: Harpers Ferry is the abolition-crisis hinge in Fuller's book, just as Origin is the scientific hinge. Brown's failed raid changes how Concord reads action, martyrdom, violence, and national judgment in the weeks before Darwin's book crosses the Atlantic.
  - Book anchor: Chapters 1, 5, and 7 repeatedly return to the raid as the moral and political background to the New Year's Origin dinner.
  - Web context: Wikipedia and the American Battlefield Trust corroborate the October 16-18 dates and summarize the raid's aim, defeat, and Civil War significance.

- **October 1859 - Sanborn burns papers, consults John A. Andrew, and flees toward Canada after the raid.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F5, F16; W-Sanborn, W-SecretSix.
  - Evidence: The event shows how Brown's action immediately endangers his supporters. Fuller uses Sanborn's panic and paper-burning to keep Brown's raid inside the domestic spaces where Origin will soon be discussed.
  - Book anchor: Chapters 5 and 16 connect Sanborn's actions to the later federal arrest attempt.
  - Web context: Sanborn and Secret Six sources identify the network the investigation was trying to expose.

- **October 30, 1859 - Thoreau first delivers "A Plea for Captain John Brown" in Concord.** Check: web-confirmed; Fuller-confirmed for the family vote and local speech scene. Sources: F7; W-PleaJohnBrown, W-Thoreau, W-JohnBrownRaid.
  - Evidence: Fuller uses Thoreau's first Brown speech to show that the naturalist who reads Darwin most deeply is also a political radical responding to slavery. Brown therefore remains in the room, conceptually, when Thoreau turns to Origin.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7 ties Thoreau's first Brown speech to his winter Darwin reading.
  - Web context: Plea, Thoreau, and raid sources corroborate the speech date and public abolitionist context.

- **November 2, 1859 - Brown is sentenced to death in Virginia.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-JohnBrownRaid, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: The sentence begins the month in which Brown becomes, for many Northern antislavery readers, a martyr rather than merely a failed raider. Fuller uses this transformation to explain Concord's emotional temperature at the turn of 1860.
  - Web context: The raid and Brown biographies corroborate the trial outcome and December execution sequence.

- **November 1859 - Thoreau repeats "A Plea for Captain John Brown" in Boston and Worcester.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed for Boston/Worcester sequence. Sources: F7; W-PleaJohnBrown, W-Thoreau, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: The repeated speeches matter because Brown's defense moves beyond Concord before the execution. Fuller treats this sequence as part of the shift by which Brown becomes a martyr for Northern abolitionists.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7 follows the speech from Concord to Boston and Worcester.
  - Web context: Plea and Thoreau sources corroborate the pre-execution repetition of the speech.

- **December 2, 1859 - Brown is hanged.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F1, F7; W-JohnBrownRaid, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: Brown's execution is one of the public dates Fuller uses to anchor the book's chronology. It falls just before Gray receives Origin and before Sanborn's New Year's dinner, binding abolition martyrdom and Darwinian controversy into the same winter.
  - Book anchor: Chapters 1 and 7 frame Thoreau and the Concord circle's winter through Brown's death.
  - Web context: Brown and raid sources confirm the execution date and its place in the aftermath of Harpers Ferry.

- **December 2, 1859 - Thoreau gives "Remarks After the Hanging of John Brown" at Concord's memorial.** Check: web-confirmed; Fuller-confirmed for the Concord memorial. Sources: F7, F1; W-RemarksJohnBrown, W-Thoreau, W-JohnBrownRaid.
  - Evidence: The memorial makes Brown's execution a Concord civic event, not only a Virginia execution. Fuller places Thoreau, Emerson, Alcott, and Sanborn together at the ritual edge between mourning and political defiance.
  - Book anchor: Chapters 1 and 7 describe the Concord response to Brown's execution.
  - Web context: Remarks and raid sources corroborate Thoreau's December 2 speech and Brown's execution.

- **December 31, 1859 - Abba Alcott reflects on Brown's martyrdom as Concord prepares for the new year.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F1; W-JohnBrown, W-AmosBronsonAlcott.
  - Evidence: Fuller uses Abba Alcott's reflection to show Brown's presence in Concord households on the eve of the Origin dinner. The point is intimate and local: national crisis has become family conversation.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 1 places this domestic moment immediately before Sanborn hosts the Darwin dinner.
  - Web context: Brown and Alcott sources supply public biography while Fuller supplies the private diary context.

- **January 16, 1860 - Sanborn receives a Senate summons to testify about Brown.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F6; W-Sanborn, W-SecretSix, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: Brown's raid continues to reach into Concord after his death. Fuller's chronology makes the summons overlap with the first American readings of Origin, so the scientific debate and federal investigation proceed at the same time.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 6 places the summons amid the Concord winter of Darwin reading and antislavery anxiety.
  - Web context: Sanborn and Secret Six sources explain why federal authorities wanted testimony from Brown's supporters.

- **Late February 1860 - Sanborn returns to Concord with Sarah and Anne Brown.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F12; W-Sanborn, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: Bringing Brown's daughters to Concord makes the raid's aftermath personal for the community. Fuller uses their presence to keep Brown's family and memory alongside the unfolding Darwin controversy.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 12 keeps Brown's daughters inside the same seasonal arc as the early Origin debates.
  - Web context: Brown sources identify Sarah and Anne within Brown's family, while Fuller supplies the Concord placement.

- **April 3, 1860 - Federal marshals attempt to arrest Sanborn over Brown's raid; Concord neighbors intervene.** Check: web-confirmed; Fuller-confirmed for local sequence. Sources: F16; W-Sanborn, W-SecretSix, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: This is Brown's aftershock made physical: the federal government reaches into Concord and the town resists. Fuller uses the scene to show how the Brown crisis fuses law, community loyalty, and radical politics in the same period as the Origin arguments.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 16 presents the arrest attempt as the dramatic local climax of Sanborn's Brown entanglement.
  - Web context: Sanborn and Secret Six sources corroborate the arrest context and Sanborn's Brown connection.

- **April 4, 1860 - Sanborn displays the handcuffs at the Concord Town House and turns Brown's lesson into a call for resistance.** Check: Fuller-confirmed for speech; web-confirmed context for arrest episode. Sources: F16; W-Sanborn, W-SecretSix, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: Sanborn's speech belongs on Brown's line because Fuller presents it as an aftershock of Harpers Ferry: the town's rescued conspirator holds up the physical sign of federal power and identifies it with slavery.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 16 follows Sanborn from the attempted arrest to the Town House speech.
  - Web context: Sanborn and Secret Six sources corroborate the arrest context and Sanborn's Brown connection.

- **June 1860 - Mary Brown and other Brown family members visit Concord.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F17; W-JohnBrown, W-Sanborn.
  - Evidence: The visit shows that Brown's memory remains socially active in Concord after the legal emergency passes. Fuller places the family visit beside literary and Darwinian developments, not as a separate story.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 17 keeps Brown's family presence in the Concord network during the summer of 1860.
  - Web context: Brown and Sanborn sources provide public context for the family and network.

- **July 4, 1860 - Thoreau reads "The Last Days of John Brown" at Brown's North Elba memorial service.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-LastDaysJohnBrown, W-Thoreau, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: The speech keeps Brown's death active after the legal crisis and before the war. It belongs here because the Brown chronology is tracking afterlife and public memory as much as Brown's own lifetime.
  - Web context: The Last Days of John Brown and Thoreau sources corroborate the July 4 memorial reading.

- **January 1, 1863 - Emancipation takes effect, and Brown's supporters read it as vindication.** Check: web-confirmed for Emancipation; Fuller-confirmed for Brown memory. Sources: F23; NatArchives-Emancipation, W-JohnBrown, W-SecretSix.
  - Evidence: Fuller treats Emancipation as an afterlife of Brown's prophecy: what looked criminal in 1859 becomes, to his admirers, a grim anticipation of national policy. The date also reminds the reader that Thoreau is already dead by this later Brown-memory chapter.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 23 places Brown's ghost inside Civil War emancipation and Concord commemoration.
  - Web context: The National Archives anchors the Emancipation Proclamation date, while Brown and Secret Six sources explain the memory network.

- **1863 - Bronson Alcott lectures on John Brown.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F23; W-AmosBronsonAlcott, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: Alcott's lecture belongs to the memory of Brown rather than the raid itself. Fuller uses it to show how Concord radicals turn Brown into a continuing moral text during the war.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 23 follows Brown's posthumous presence in Concord lectures and commemorations.
  - Web context: Alcott and Brown sources provide public biographical context; Fuller supplies the lecture's placement in the book.

- **1885 - Sanborn publishes Life and Letters of John Brown.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F23; W-Sanborn, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: Sanborn's biography turns participant memory into printed historical memory. Fuller uses the later publication to show how Brown remains central to Sanborn's identity long after Darwin's book and the Civil War have moved into retrospect.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 23 treats Sanborn as one of Brown's custodians in memory.
  - Web context: Sanborn and Brown sources corroborate Sanborn's long association with Brown.

- **1916-1917 - Sanborn still publicly honors Brown shortly before his own death.** Check: Fuller-confirmed; web-confirmed for Sanborn's death. Sources: F23; W-Sanborn, W-JohnBrown.
  - Evidence: This late endpoint makes clear why Fuller's chapter order can move far beyond Thoreau's death: Brown's afterlife extends through Sanborn's nearly century-long life. The chronology separates that afterlife from the 1860 Origin reception.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 23 carries Brown memory into Sanborn's final years.
  - Web context: Sanborn's biography confirms his 1917 death, while Brown's biography anchors the figure being commemorated.
