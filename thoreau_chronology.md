# Henry David Thoreau Chronology

Source keys are defined in `the_book_that_changed_america_chronology.md`.
This chronology is sorted by event date, not by the order in which Fuller
discusses Thoreau.

## Chronology

- **July 12, 1817 - Thoreau is born in Concord, Massachusetts.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Thoreau.
  - Summary: Thoreau was born in Concord, the town that became the center of his writing, natural history, politics, and final illness. Fuller's chronology repeatedly returns to Concord as Thoreau's field site and moral community.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau.

- **1837 - Thoreau graduates from Harvard, meets Emerson more fully, and begins the journal that will become central to his life as a naturalist and writer.** Check: web-confirmed for Harvard/Emerson/journal context; Fuller-confirmed for its role in this book's arc. Sources: F7; W-Thoreau, W-Emerson.
  - Summary: Thoreau graduated from Harvard, deepened his contact with Emerson, and began the journal that became his lifelong record of observation. That journal later preserves the natural-history work that makes his Darwin reading so important.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7: The Nick of Time (text book.md:581-658; notes book.md:2141-2218).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau; Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **1841 - Thoreau lives with the Emerson family.** Check: web-confirmed context. Sources: F7; W-Thoreau, W-Emerson.
  - Summary: Thoreau lived in the Emerson household and entered the orbit of Concord Transcendentalism more fully. The arrangement tied his daily life to Emerson's intellectual circle.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7: The Nick of Time (text book.md:581-658; notes book.md:2141-2218).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau; Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **January 1842 - John Thoreau dies after tetanus/lockjaw, an event Fuller treats as formative for Henry's later emotional and intellectual life.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F7; W-Thoreau.
  - Summary: John Thoreau died after contracting lockjaw, a loss that profoundly affected Henry. Fuller treats the death as one of the emotional facts behind Thoreau's later attention to mortality, nature, and transformation.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7: The Nick of Time (text book.md:581-658; notes book.md:2141-2218).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau.

- **July 4, 1845 - Thoreau moves to Walden Pond on Emerson's land.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F7; W-Thoreau, W-Walden.
  - Summary: Thoreau moved to a cabin at Walden Pond on Emerson's land. The experiment became the basis for Walden and for his public image as a writer of nature and self-reliance.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7: The Nick of Time (text book.md:581-658; notes book.md:2141-2218).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau; Wikipedia, Walden.

- **July 24 or 25, 1846 - Thoreau is jailed after refusing to pay the poll tax.** Check: web-confirmed for date range; Fuller-confirmed for its placement in the antislavery arc. Sources: F7; W-Thoreau.
  - Summary: Thoreau spent a night in jail after refusing to pay the poll tax. The episode became part of the political experience behind his later essay on resistance to unjust government.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7: The Nick of Time (text book.md:581-658; notes book.md:2141-2218).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau.

- **January-February 1848 - Thoreau lectures at the Concord Lyceum on the rights and duties of the individual toward government.** Check: web-confirmed for lecture/publication sequence; Fuller-confirmed for its later antislavery placement. Sources: F7; W-CivilDisobedience, W-Thoreau.
  - Summary: Thoreau lectured at the Concord Lyceum on individual conscience and government. The lectures led toward the essay later known as Civil Disobedience.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7: The Nick of Time (text book.md:581-658; notes book.md:2141-2218).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Civil Disobedience; Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau.

- **1849 - A Week on the Concord and Merrimack Rivers appears, and "Resistance to Civil Government" enters print.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Thoreau, W-AWeek.
  - Summary: Thoreau published A Week and the essay Resistance to Civil Government. The year put both his literary ambitions and his theory of political refusal into print.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau; Wikipedia, A Week on the Concord and Merrimack Rivers.

- **1851-1854 - Thoreau increasingly turns toward detailed natural-history observation and reads Darwin's Voyage of the Beagle as part of that shift.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F7; W-Thoreau.
  - Summary: Thoreau moved deeper into natural-history observation and read Darwin's Voyage of the Beagle. That earlier Darwin encounter prepared him to recognize the force of Origin in 1860.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7: The Nick of Time (text book.md:581-658; notes book.md:2141-2218).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau.

- **July 4, 1854 - Thoreau delivers "Slavery in Massachusetts" at an antislavery rally in Framingham.** Check: web-confirmed; Fuller-confirmed for the antislavery arc leading to Brown. Sources: F7; W-SlaveryMassachusetts, W-Thoreau.
  - Summary: Thoreau delivered Slavery in Massachusetts at an antislavery meeting in Framingham. The speech attacked Northern complicity and shows his politics becoming more publicly abolitionist before Brown.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7: The Nick of Time (text book.md:581-658; notes book.md:2141-2218).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Slavery in Massachusetts; Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau.

- **August 9, 1854 - Walden is published.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-Thoreau, W-Walden.
  - Summary: Walden was published, turning Thoreau's pond experiment into a book. Its publication made him a public writer before his later Darwinian natural-history work matured.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau; Wikipedia, Walden.

- **1857 - Thoreau's experiments and observations on sap, seeds, distribution, and seasonal timing prepare the ground for his later Darwinian reading.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed private/book-specific date or placement. Sources: F7, F14; W-Thoreau, Gutenberg-Succession.
  - Summary: Thoreau studied sap, seeds, distribution, and seasonal change in detail. These observations built the empirical habits that made Origin feel like a jolt of recognition rather than an abstraction.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7: The Nick of Time (text book.md:581-658; notes book.md:2141-2218); Chapter 14: A Jolt of Recognition (text book.md:1007-1078; notes book.md:2519-2566).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau; Project Gutenberg, Thoreau's "The Succession of Forest Trees."

- **October 30, 1859 - Thoreau first delivers "A Plea for Captain John Brown" in Concord.** Check: web-confirmed for first delivery; Fuller-confirmed for family vote and local effect. Sources: F7; W-PleaJohnBrown, W-Thoreau, W-JohnBrownRaid.
  - Summary: Thoreau delivered A Plea for Captain John Brown in Concord shortly after the raid. He defended Brown as a man of conscience when many public voices condemned him.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7: The Nick of Time (text book.md:581-658; notes book.md:2141-2218).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, A Plea for Captain John Brown; Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau; Wikipedia, John Brown's raid on Harpers Ferry.

- **November 1859 - Thoreau repeats "A Plea for Captain John Brown" in Boston and Worcester.** Check: web-confirmed context; Fuller-confirmed for Boston/Worcester sequence. Sources: F7; W-PleaJohnBrown, W-Thoreau.
  - Summary: Thoreau repeated his Brown speech in Boston and Worcester. The repetitions widened his public defense of Brown before the execution.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7: The Nick of Time (text book.md:581-658; notes book.md:2141-2218).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, A Plea for Captain John Brown; Wikipedia, Henry David Thoreau.

- **December 2, 1859 - Brown is hanged; Fuller frames Thoreau's winter Darwin reading against this event.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F1, F7; W-JohnBrownRaid.
  - Summary: Brown was hanged on December 2, 1859, just before Origin began circulating through Fuller's Concord network. The execution set the moral atmosphere for Thoreau's winter Darwin reading.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 1: The Book from Across the Atlantic (text book.md:235-294; notes book.md:1847-1886); Chapter 7: The Nick of Time (text book.md:581-658; notes book.md:2141-2218).

  - Summary: Thoreau spoke at Concord's Brown memorial on the day of the hanging. His remarks turned the execution into a local ritual of antislavery mourning and defiance.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 7: The Nick of Time (text book.md:581-658; notes book.md:2141-2218); Chapter 1: The Book from Across the Atlantic (text book.md:235-294; notes book.md:1847-1886).

## Arc in Fuller

Fuller's Thoreau is the Concord reader who carries Darwin furthest into
practice. The order matters: Thoreau's deepest Origin work happens in 1860-1861,
and his death in 1862 must be placed before the later Sanborn, Brace, Gray, and
Alcott afterlives discussed in chapters 22-23.
