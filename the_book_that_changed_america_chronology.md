# Chronology for Randall Fuller's The Book That Changed America

Created: 2026-07-01.

Source book: <https://closereading.rahuldave.us/books/125>

This chronology is sorted by event date, not by chapter order. The book keys
below point to the converted Markdown chapter text and the corresponding notes
section in Fuller's book. The web keys are corroborating external checks,
including Wikipedia and non-Wikipedia sources. Private letters, journal entries,
Concord conversations, and Fuller's interpretive claims remain sourced primarily
to Fuller.

Date-check policy:

- **Web-confirmed** means the event's date or year is corroborated by an external source.
- **Web-confirmed context** means an external source corroborates the public
  person, event, publication, organization, or historical frame, while Fuller
  supplies a private date, local scene, or book-specific interpretation.
- **Fuller-confirmed** means Fuller gives the exact date for a private/journal/letter event; web sources only corroborate surrounding biography or public context.
- **Web-context** means the web confirms the person, publication, organization, or broader episode, while Fuller supplies this book's specific interpretive placement.

Chapter-order warning: Fuller sometimes discusses later historical afterlives
before returning to earlier Thoreau material. For example, Sanborn's postwar and
twentieth-century afterlife in chapter 23 is sorted here after Thoreau's death
in 1862, even though chapter 24 then returns to Thoreau's final 1860-1862 arc.

## High-Level Chapter Flow

The sorted chronology above also follows a chapter-by-chapter arc:

- **Chapters 1-4:** Darwin's book travels from Gray to Brace to Concord while Brown's execution dominates the moral atmosphere.
- **Chapters 5-7:** Sanborn, Alcott, Higginson, and Thoreau bring antislavery politics and transcendental natural history into the first American reading of Origin.
- **Chapters 8-11:** Gorillas, Agassiz, Barnum, Brace, and Gray turn Origin into a public fight over race, human unity, and scientific authority.
- **Chapters 12-15:** Louisa May Alcott and Thoreau absorb Darwin into antislavery fiction, wild apples, forest succession, and the problem of destruction and regeneration.
- **Chapters 16-21:** Sanborn's arrest, the Democratic split, the Hawthorne return, Moods, Gray's Atlantic essays, and Thoreau's "Succession" lecture show Origin moving through politics, literature, theology, and ecology.
- **Chapters 22-24:** Wallace, Brace, Gray, Louisa, Sanborn, and Thoreau's final months show how 1860 continued to unfold through war, emancipation, evolutionary theology, and Concord memory.
