# Amos Bronson Alcott Chronology

Source keys are defined in `the_book_that_changed_america_chronology.md`.
Bronson Alcott is one of the four people at Sanborn's January 1, 1860 Origin
dinner.

## Chronology

- **November 29, 1799 - Amos Bronson Alcott is born in Wolcott, Connecticut.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-AmosBronsonAlcott.
  - Summary: Alcott was born in Connecticut and became one of the central educational and spiritual reformers of the Transcendentalist circle. His later reactions to Darwin come from this idealist and pedagogical background.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Amos Bronson Alcott.

- **May 22, 1830 - Alcott marries Abby May.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: W-AmosBronsonAlcott.
  - Summary: Alcott married Abby May, usually called Abba in the family context. Their household became the domestic world of Louisa May Alcott and an important node in Concord reform culture.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Amos Bronson Alcott.

- **September 22, 1834 - Alcott opens the Temple School in Boston.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F6; W-AmosBronsonAlcott, W-TempleSchool.
  - Summary: Alcott opened the Temple School, where he experimented with conversational and child-centered pedagogy. The school made him famous and controversial as an educational reformer.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 6: A Night at the Lyceum (text book.md:501-580; notes book.md:2067-2140).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Amos Bronson Alcott; Wikipedia, Temple School.

- **1835 - Elizabeth Palmer Peabody publishes Record of a School about Alcott's Temple School.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F6; W-AmosBronsonAlcott, W-TempleSchool.
  - Summary: Peabody's Record of a School presented Alcott's teaching methods to a wider public. The book helped advertise both the promise and the strangeness of his educational experiment.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 6: A Night at the Lyceum (text book.md:501-580; notes book.md:2067-2140).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Amos Bronson Alcott; Wikipedia, Temple School.

- **Late 1836 - Alcott's Conversations with Children on the Gospels appears and provokes public controversy.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F6; W-AmosBronsonAlcott, W-TempleSchool.
  - Summary: Alcott's Conversations with Children on the Gospels brought public criticism because of its religious frankness with children. The controversy damaged the Temple School and marked Alcott as a radical educator.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 6: A Night at the Lyceum (text book.md:501-580; notes book.md:2067-2140).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Amos Bronson Alcott; Wikipedia, Temple School.

- **1836 - Alcott becomes part of the Transcendentalist circle around Emerson.** Check: web-confirmed context. Sources: F6; W-AmosBronsonAlcott, W-Emerson.
  - Summary: Alcott entered the Transcendentalist circle gathered around Emerson. His spiritualized view of development later shaped his resistance to Darwin's material account of natural change.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 6: A Night at the Lyceum (text book.md:501-580; notes book.md:2067-2140).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Amos Bronson Alcott; Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **1840 - The Alcott family moves near Emerson in Concord.** Check: web-confirmed context. Sources: F6; W-AmosBronsonAlcott, W-Emerson.
  - Summary: The Alcott family moved to Concord near Emerson. That move placed Alcott inside the town network that later includes Thoreau, Sanborn, Brown memorials, and the Origin dinner.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 6: A Night at the Lyceum (text book.md:501-580; notes book.md:2067-2140).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Amos Bronson Alcott; Wikipedia, Ralph Waldo Emerson.

- **1843-1844 - Alcott's Fruitlands experiment with Charles Lane begins and fails.** Check: web-confirmed. Sources: F6; W-AmosBronsonAlcott, W-Fruitlands.
  - Summary: Alcott and Charles Lane attempted to build Fruitlands as a communal, ascetic reform experiment. It failed quickly, but it remained a defining example of Alcott's willingness to test ideals in daily life.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 6: A Night at the Lyceum (text book.md:501-580; notes book.md:2067-2140).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Amos Bronson Alcott; Wikipedia, Fruitlands.

- **1859 - Alcott serves as superintendent of Concord schools.** Check: web-confirmed context. Sources: W-AmosBronsonAlcott.
  - Summary: Alcott served as superintendent of Concord schools. The office placed him in a practical educational role just before the Origin winter and the Brown crisis reached the town.
  - Web context: Wikipedia, Amos Bronson Alcott.

- **December 31, 1859 - Abba Alcott reflects on John Brown's martyrdom as the family and Concord prepare for the new year.** Check: Fuller-confirmed family/journal context; web-confirmed for Brown execution. Sources: F1; W-JohnBrownRaid, W-AmosBronsonAlcott.
  - Summary: Abba Alcott reflected on Brown's death as martyrdom at the end of 1859. Fuller uses the diary moment to show Brown's execution entering Concord family life before Darwin enters the parlor.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 1: The Book from Across the Atlantic (text book.md:235-294; notes book.md:1847-1886).
  - Web context: Wikipedia, John Brown's raid on Harpers Ferry; Wikipedia, Amos Bronson Alcott.

  - Summary: Alcott attended Sanborn's New Year dinner, where Brace brought Gray's marked copy of Origin. He was present at the book's symbolic arrival among Fuller's Concord readers.
  - Book anchor: Chapter 1: The Book from Across the Atlantic (text book.md:235-294; notes book.md:1847-1886); Chapter 6: A Night at the Lyceum (text book.md:501-580; notes book.md:2067-2140).

## Arc in Fuller

Alcott receives Darwin as a spiritual and philosophical challenge rather than a
scientific program. Fuller uses him as a foil to Thoreau: both are in the same
Concord rooms, but Thoreau turns Origin into observation and ecological method,
while Alcott tries to absorb it into a preexisting idealist cosmology.
